Titcomb Lakes, Winifred Peak, and Stroud Peak

7/25/2025 - 7/27/2025 - Titcomb Lakes, Winifred Peak, and Stroud Peak

This was a really enjoyable three day, two night backpacking trip through the popular Titcomb Lake area with an off trail route over Knapsack Col to the Green River headwaters basin. The trail started at Elkhart trailhead and went through dense forest for about 8.5 miles to Seneca lake. At that point, the scenery became more open and improved quite a bit. The next couple miles to Island Lake weaved through wildflower fields and short hills. At the lake, the Titcomb lake area came into view to the north with glacier covered peaks in the distance. I headed this way but made a little detour to an area called Indian Basin, exiting the trail and following a creek for a mile or so up a couple hundred feet. Indian basin was nice and quiet and had a few lakes. But I was running short on daylight and headed back to the trail in the direction of the Titcomb basin. I followed along a series of connected lakes surrounded by towering peaks. This was probably the highlight of the trip. There was a nice area next to a creek with a flat site where I set up my tent and quickly got inside to avoid clouds of mosquitos. The next morning I started around 7 am and continued walking north as the trail fizzled out and was replaced by creek hopping and scree. At the end of the basin I turned left and hiked up about 1,400 ft of talus and scree to Knapsack Col, passing patches of snow and fast flowing glacier melt water. Atop the couloir I rested then hiked up about 440 ft to Winifred Peak which offered nice views of the basin I walked through and the basin over the other side of the ridge that I could now see. I headed down and over to the other side, navigating over boulders and rocks, and the headwaters of the Green River which flows out of the mountain range and for 730 miles through Wyoming and Utah before emptying into the Colorado River. I passed connected teal hue ponds and streams before the valley narrowed and became slightly marshy. Shortly after, at Peak Lake, I eyed Stroud Peak and decided to make an ascent. From lake level there was but a limited view. I could only see an initial hill that looked steep but doable. After I climbed the real challenge came into view. There was a large boulder field for about .40 miles and 650 ft of elevation gain. It didn’t look bad but was extremely slow going and took over two hours to ascend to the saddle just below Stroud Peak. It was towards the end of the day and a long hiking day at that, and several times I thought about turning back but kept going. Finally, I reached the saddle, then made a quick .25 mile and 350 ft gain to the summit. There was a sheer drop on three sides but the view was great. To the north was the backside of Squaretop mountain and the Green River Valley. It was now late in the afternoon and I quickly descended the peak from a different and much easier route down, it only taking a half hour. I walked through pleasant fields and grass along streams for an hour, saw an overweight marmot sun basking on a boulder, then set up camp by Upper Jean Lake. The hike out the next morning started very nicely with a continuation of lake and streams with jagged peaks in the background. A few miles later I entered the forest along the Highline trail and walked the remaining 13 miles back to the car.
