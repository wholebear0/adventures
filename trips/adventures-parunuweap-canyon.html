Adventures - Parunuweap Canyon

5/10/25 - Parunuweap Canyon

Before emptying into the Virgin River, the clear blue waters of the South Fork wind through Parunuweap Canyon from the east near Kanab across Zion to private land in Springdale, the town just outside Zion. The river is seldom visited and difficult to reach, with only a few access points. One is a 25 mile 4x4 road through sand, the other is a multi-day backpack beginning near the canyon head near Kanab, and the last is to start in Zion at Checkerboard Mesa and hike 6 miles down to the river which is what I did. The canyon had been on my Zion hike bucket list for at least two years and I was excited for the adventure and unknown. The first half of the way to the river followed along an easy to follow social trail, first passing through a narrow and lush temperate canyon, climbing to a saddle point, then a 4 mile descent across open slickrock dotted with Juniper and Ponderosa. A mile or so before the river I caught an aroma of the water in the air and knew it was close. An overlook was reached but the descent down was steep and required stemming between rocks to lower myself down. At this point I could see the canyon floor and river though wasnt sure exactly where I would end up. The descent was like a plane preparing for a landing, banking right and left while navigating down a web of slickrock and boulders, then finally touching canyon bottom. Surrounding was rushing blue river and bright green trees and bushes, outlined by sheer canyon walls eroded away over millions of years by the force of the river. It was really something. I put on my hip waders and hiked upriver against the strong current which proved to be a workout. On average the water was calf height but reached up to hip level and higher. I passed through narrows and small falls but was ultimately stopped a mile and a half up by a big boulder and waterfall. I turned around and went back past where I got to the river but was blocked by another set of falls that way too. On the way back there was a plaque dedicated in the 70s to John Powell who navigated the river in the 1800s. The hike out was hot and tiring. Not realizing it, I had drank over 2 of the 3 liters of water so rationing was needed. After a long day of hiking I got back to the car at 6 pm and opened the doors to an oven inside. The spare water I had in the car was as hot as a bath but still refreshing.
